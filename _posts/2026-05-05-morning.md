### We say 'rich as Croesus' and 'touch gold' because Croesus was a legendary king of Lydia in ancient Greece known for his immense wealth, and pactole is an old French word for gold.


{% include figure.html
    src="https://www.ici.fr/s3/cruiser-production-eu3/2026/05/6f8efe9d-2d67-4498-bba8-af7fc4d6aa52/464x261_sc_gettyimages-163753664.jpg"
    alt="We say 'rich as Croesus' and 'touch gold' because Croesus was a legendary king of Lydia in ancient Greece known for his immense wealth, and pactole is an old French word for gold."
    caption="Pourquoi dit-on 'riche comme Crésus' et 'toucher le pactole' ?" %}


The term "rich as Croesus" and "touch the pactolus" are ancient Greek expressions that have been incorporated into the French language.

Croesus was a king of Lydia, a region in Asia Minor, in the 6th century B.C. He was known for his immense wealth, particularly his gold collection. Thus, the expression "rich as Croesus" has been used to describe someone who possesses a considerable fortune.

The word "pactolus," on the other hand, is derived from the name of the Pactolus River, also located in Asia Minor. This river was famous for its gold-bearing sands, giving rise to the term "pactolus" to denote gold or a source of gold.

Croesus' story is marked by an unexpected overthrow. Although he was considered the richest king of his time, he was defeated by King Cyrus II of Persia and saw his fortune collapse. Furthermore, the Oracle of Delos had predicted that his empire would never fall, which was considered a false prophecy.

In summary, the terms "rich as Croesus" and "touch the pactolus" are ancient Greek expressions that have been integrated into French to denote a considerable fortune and gold or a source of gold. The story of Croesus is marked by an unexpected overthrow, with his defeat at the hands of the Persians and the Oracle of Delos' false prophecy that his empire would never fall.

### 'We are not like the rest of Canada': Alberta stands apart for having its independence referendum.


The government of Alberta has announced its intention to hold a referendum on the province's independence, which would be a first in Canadian history. This decision is motivated by growing disagreements between Alberta and the federal government over energy and fiscal policies. Alberta, rich in oil resources, seeks more control over its own industry and more revenue to fund social programs. However, the federal government has declared that an independence referendum is not legal and that the Canadian Constitution does not allow a province to separate from Canada without the agreement of all other provinces and Parliament. Supporters of independence argue that Alberta is different from other provinces due to its economic dependence on oil production, which represents more than 40% of its revenues. They also claim that Alberta has been negatively affected by federal policies on climate and the environment, which have resulted in restrictions on oil production. Opponents of independence highlight that Alberta heavily relies on financial aid from the federal government for social programs and infrastructure, and that secession could have negative effects on the Canadian economy. Discussions between the Alberta government and the federal government are ongoing to find a solution to these differences.
